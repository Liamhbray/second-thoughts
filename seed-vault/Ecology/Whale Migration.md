# Whale Migration

Most large whale species undertake annual migrations between high-latitude feeding grounds and low-latitude breeding grounds — among the longest migrations of any mammal.

## Pattern

The general pattern is:
- **Summer** (polar): feed intensively in cold, nutrient-rich waters where [[Krill and Prey Ecology|krill]] and fish are abundant
- **Winter** (tropical): breed and calve in warm, predator-poor waters where calves can build fat reserves

[[Humpback Whales]] migrate up to 8,000 km each way. [[Blue Whales]] follow similar routes but are less predictable. [[Sperm Whales]] are partial migrants — females stay in warm waters year-round while males migrate to polar feeding grounds.

## Navigation

How whales navigate across featureless ocean remains poorly understood. Hypotheses include:
- Earth's magnetic field (magnetoreception)
- [[Ocean Currents]] and temperature gradients
- Acoustic landmarks (undersea topography reflecting sound)
- Cultural memory passed through generations (see [[Whale Communication]])

## Ecological Significance

Migration connects ecosystems. Whales feed at the poles and defecate in the tropics, transporting nitrogen, iron, and phosphorus across ocean basins. This "great whale conveyor belt" fertilises nutrient-poor tropical waters — part of the broader [[Whale Pump]] process.

## Threats

Migration routes are increasingly disrupted by shipping lanes, naval exercises, and shifting prey distributions due to climate change. See [[Threats to Whales]].
