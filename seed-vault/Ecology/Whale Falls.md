# Whale Falls

When a whale dies and sinks to the deep ocean floor, its carcass creates a localised ecosystem called a whale fall. These events sustain unique communities of organisms for decades.

## Succession Stages

A whale fall passes through distinct ecological phases:

### Mobile Scavenger Stage (months to years)
Hagfish, sleeper sharks, and large crustaceans consume the soft tissue. A single [[Blue Whales|blue whale]] carcass can provide 40-60 tonnes of organic matter to the deep-sea floor.

### Enrichment Opportunist Stage (months to years)
Polychaete worms and crustaceans colonise the bones and surrounding sediment, feeding on residual organic matter.

### Sulphophilic Stage (decades)
Anaerobic bacteria decompose the lipid-rich bones, producing hydrogen sulphide. This supports chemosynthetic communities — similar to hydrothermal vent ecosystems. Specialised species including bone-eating *Osedax* worms are found only at whale falls.

## Ecological Significance

Whale falls may serve as stepping stones for deep-sea species, connecting isolated chemosynthetic ecosystems (vents, seeps) across the ocean floor.

The frequency of whale falls has declined dramatically due to [[Whaling History]]. A healthy ocean would produce roughly 70,000 whale falls per year — the current rate is a fraction of this, potentially isolating deep-sea communities.

## Connection to the Whale Pump

Whale falls are the terminal stage of the [[Whale Pump]] — the final transfer of surface-derived carbon and nutrients to the deep ocean, where they are sequestered for centuries.
