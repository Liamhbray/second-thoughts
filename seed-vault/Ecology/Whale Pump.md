# Whale Pump

The whale pump is the mechanism by which whales transport nutrients vertically and horizontally through the ocean, stimulating primary productivity.

## Vertical Cycling

Whales feed at depth and defecate at or near the surface. Their faecal plumes are rich in iron and nitrogen — limiting nutrients for phytoplankton growth. This "biological pump" is especially significant in iron-limited waters like the Southern Ocean.

[[Sperm Whales]] are the most effective vertical pumpers: they feed on squid at 1,000+ metres (see [[Whale Diving]]) and defecate at the surface, transporting nutrients across the full water column.

## Horizontal Transport

[[Whale Migration]] moves nutrients across ocean basins. Whales feed in nutrient-rich polar waters and release nutrients in nutrient-poor tropical waters through defecation, urine, sloughed skin, and placental material. This "great whale conveyor belt" subsidises tropical ecosystems.

## Carbon Sequestration

The whale pump enhances carbon capture in two ways:
1. **Fertilisation**: whale faeces stimulate phytoplankton blooms that absorb CO₂
2. **[[Whale Falls]]**: when whales die, their carbon-rich bodies sink to the deep ocean floor, sequestering carbon for centuries

Estimates suggest that restoring whale populations to pre-whaling levels could capture tens of thousands of tonnes of carbon annually.

## Connection to Krill

The whale pump creates a positive feedback loop with [[Krill and Prey Ecology|krill]]: whales eat krill, their faeces fertilise phytoplankton, phytoplankton feeds krill, krill feeds whales. The removal of whales through [[Whaling History]] disrupted this cycle.
