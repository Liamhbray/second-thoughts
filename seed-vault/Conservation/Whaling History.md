# Whaling History

Commercial whaling over three centuries killed an estimated 3 million great whales, driving multiple species to the brink of extinction and fundamentally altering ocean ecosystems.

## Timeline

### Shore Whaling (1600s-1800s)
Basque, Dutch, and American whalers hunted right whales and [[Humpback Whales]] from small boats near coastlines. Products: oil for lamps, baleen for corsets.

### Pelagic Whaling (1800s-1860s)
The American whaling fleet hunted [[Sperm Whales]] across all oceans for spermaceti oil (premium lubricant and lamp fuel) and ambergris (perfume fixative). Nantucket and New Bedford were the industry's centres. This era inspired Melville's *Moby-Dick*.

### Industrial Whaling (1900s-1986)
Steam-powered catcher boats and explosive harpoons made it possible to hunt fast-swimming [[Blue Whales]] and fin whales. Factory ships processed carcasses at sea. Antarctic whaling peaked in the 1930s, killing 30,000+ whales per season.

By the 1960s, [[Blue Whales]] had been reduced from ~250,000 to fewer than 10,000.

## The Moratorium

The International Whaling Commission imposed a commercial whaling moratorium in 1986. Japan, Norway, and Iceland continued whaling under various exceptions. Japan's "scientific whaling" programme ended in 2019.

## Ecological Legacy

The removal of great whales disrupted the [[Whale Pump]], reduced [[Whale Falls]], and paradoxically contributed to declines in [[Krill and Prey Ecology|krill populations]]. Recovery has been uneven — [[Humpback Whales]] are rebounding, while right whales remain critically endangered.

See [[Marine Protected Areas]] and [[Threats to Whales]] for current conservation efforts.
