# Threats to Whales

Despite the end of most commercial whaling, whale populations face a complex web of modern threats. Many are synergistic — their combined effect exceeds the sum of individual impacts.

## Ship Strikes

Large vessels kill an estimated 20,000 whales per year worldwide. Right whales and [[Blue Whales]] are particularly vulnerable in coastal shipping lanes that overlap with feeding and migration routes. Speed restrictions in [[Marine Protected Areas]] reduce strike mortality by up to 80%.

## Entanglement

Fishing gear — especially lobster pot lines and gill nets — entangles an estimated 300,000 cetaceans annually. Entangled whales may drown immediately or drag gear for months, suffering chronic injury and starvation. [[Humpback Whales]] and right whales are most affected.

## Ocean Noise

Shipping, military sonar, seismic surveys, and offshore construction produce noise that masks [[Whale Communication]], disrupts feeding, and causes chronic stress. Low-frequency noise from large vessels overlaps directly with [[Blue Whales|blue whale]] and fin whale call frequencies.

## Climate Change

Warming oceans are shifting [[Ocean Currents]] and upwelling patterns, moving prey distributions away from traditional feeding grounds. Sea ice loss in the Arctic and Antarctic reduces habitat for [[Krill and Prey Ecology|krill]], the foundation of the [[Baleen Whales|baleen whale]] food web.

## Pollution

Chemical pollutants (PCBs, heavy metals) bioaccumulate in whale blubber. Plastic ingestion is increasingly documented. These chronic stressors reduce reproductive success and immune function.

## What Helps

- Expanding and enforcing [[Marine Protected Areas]]
- Ship speed restrictions and route adjustments
- Ropeless fishing gear to eliminate entanglement
- Reducing ocean noise pollution
- Restoring whale populations to rebuild the [[Whale Pump]]

The fundamental insight from [[Whaling History]] is that whale populations can recover — but only if threats are reduced faster than they accumulate.
