# Humpback Whales

Humpback whales (*Megaptera novaeangliae*) are among the most studied and charismatic cetaceans. They are known for their acrobatic breaching, complex songs, and cooperative feeding behaviour.

## Bubble Netting

Humpbacks are the only whales known to cooperatively hunt using bubble nets. A group of 5-15 whales coordinates: one blows a rising spiral of bubbles while others vocalise to herd prey. The group then lunges upward through the concentrated prey. This behaviour is culturally learned, not innate — a key example of [[Whale Communication]] driving cooperative strategy.

See [[Baleen Whales]] for context on baleen feeding mechanics.

## Song

Male humpbacks produce elaborate songs lasting 10-20 minutes, repeated for hours. All males in a population sing the same song, which evolves progressively over the breeding season. Songs spread between populations — a song originating in eastern Australia was observed propagating eastward across the Pacific over several years.

This is one of the strongest examples of non-human cultural transmission (see [[Whale Communication]]).

## Migration

Humpbacks undertake some of the longest migrations of any mammal — up to 8,000 km each way between polar feeding grounds and tropical breeding grounds. See [[Whale Migration]] for details.

## Conservation

Humpback populations have recovered significantly since the end of commercial whaling. Several populations have been delisted from endangered status, making them a [[Whaling History]] success story. They are a flagship species for [[Marine Protected Areas]].
