# Whale Communication

Whales produce some of the most complex vocalisations in the animal kingdom. Communication serves social bonding, navigation, prey detection, mating, and cultural transmission.

## Baleen Whale Song

[[Humpback Whales]] produce the most elaborate songs — structured sequences of themes lasting 10-20 minutes. All males in a population sing the same evolving song. Songs propagate between populations over years, demonstrating cultural diffusion.

[[Blue Whales]] produce low-frequency calls (10-40 Hz) that travel thousands of kilometres. Over recent decades, blue whale call frequencies have been dropping worldwide — the cause remains uncertain. Hypotheses include increasing ocean noise, population recovery, or shifts in [[Ocean Currents]] affecting sound propagation.

## Toothed Whale Vocalisations

[[Toothed Whales]] use three categories of sound:
1. **Echolocation clicks**: high-frequency pulses for hunting and navigation
2. **Burst pulses**: rapid click trains used in social contexts
3. **Whistles**: individually distinctive "signature whistles" in dolphins; coda patterns in [[Sperm Whales]]

Sperm whale codas function as cultural identity markers — different clans use different coda repertoires, passed from mother to calf.

## Threats

Anthropogenic ocean noise from shipping, sonar, and seismic surveys interferes with whale communication. Low-frequency noise masks baleen whale calls; military sonar has been linked to mass strandings of beaked whales. See [[Threats to Whales]].
