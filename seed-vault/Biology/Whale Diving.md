# Whale Diving

Cetaceans have evolved remarkable adaptations for diving to extreme depths. The challenges — oxygen depletion, crushing pressure, nitrogen narcosis, and decompression — are solved through physiology rather than equipment.

## Adaptations

- **Oxygen storage**: whales store 80-90% of oxygen in blood and muscle (via haemoglobin and myoglobin), compared to ~35% in human lungs. This allows them to collapse their lungs at depth without losing access to oxygen.
- **Bradycardia**: heart rate drops dramatically during a dive (to 4-8 bpm in [[Blue Whales]]), reducing oxygen consumption.
- **Selective vasoconstriction**: blood flow is redirected away from non-essential organs toward the brain and heart.
- **Flexible ribcage**: ribs are loosely attached to the spine, allowing the chest to compress at depth without fracturing. [[Sperm Whales]] can withstand pressures exceeding 200 atmospheres.
- **Nitrogen management**: lung collapse prevents nitrogen from entering the bloodstream, avoiding decompression sickness.

## Depth Records

| Species | Typical Depth | Maximum Recorded |
|---------|--------------|-----------------|
| [[Sperm Whales]] | 1,000-2,000 m | 2,250 m |
| Cuvier's beaked whale | 1,000-1,500 m | 2,992 m (deepest mammal) |
| [[Humpback Whales]] | 50-200 m | 616 m |
| [[Blue Whales]] | 100-300 m | 506 m |

## Ecological Role

Deep-diving whales transport nutrients vertically through the water column. Their faecal plumes at the surface fertilise phytoplankton — a process called the [[Whale Pump]].
