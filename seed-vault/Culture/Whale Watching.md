# Whale Watching

Whale watching is the observation of cetaceans in their natural habitat, practised commercially in over 100 countries. It generates an estimated $2.1 billion annually — demonstrating that live whales have far greater economic value than dead ones.

## History

Commercial whale watching began in the 1950s in San Diego, California. It expanded rapidly after the 1986 whaling moratorium, as former whaling communities transitioned to tourism-based economies. Iceland, once a whaling nation, now earns more from whale watching than it ever did from whaling.

## Key Species and Locations

- **[[Humpback Whales]]**: the most popular species due to breaching and surface behaviour. Best locations: Maui (Hawaii), Hervey Bay (Australia), Samaná Bay (Dominican Republic)
- **[[Blue Whales]]**: Monterey Bay (California), Mirissa (Sri Lanka), Azores
- **[[Sperm Whales]]**: Kaikōura (New Zealand), Dominica, Azores
- **Orcas**: Salish Sea (Pacific Northwest), Tromsø (Norway)

## Impact on Conservation

Whale watching builds public support for [[Marine Protected Areas]] and conservation funding. Communities that depend on whale tourism become advocates for habitat protection and [[Threats to Whales|threat reduction]].

## Concerns

Poorly regulated whale watching can itself become a threat — engine noise disrupts [[Whale Communication]], repeated close approaches cause chronic stress, and propeller strikes injure whales. Best practices include maintaining minimum distances, limiting time near animals, and reducing speed.
