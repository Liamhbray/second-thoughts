# Moby-Dick

Herman Melville's *Moby-Dick* (1851) is the defining work of whale literature — a novel about obsession, nature, and the limits of human understanding, set against the backdrop of the American [[Whaling History|whaling industry]].

## Synopsis

Captain Ahab, commanding the whaling ship Pequod, pursues a white [[Sperm Whales|sperm whale]] that severed his leg on a previous voyage. Narrator Ishmael observes as Ahab's monomaniacal quest consumes the ship and crew.

## Cetology

Melville devotes entire chapters to whale biology, anatomy, and taxonomy — remarkably accurate for the 1850s. His descriptions of sperm whale diving behaviour anticipate modern research on [[Whale Diving]], and his account of the whale's "battering ram" forehead aligns with current understanding of the spermaceti organ.

## Themes

- **Nature as unknowable**: the white whale resists all symbolic interpretation — it simply *is*
- **Obsession vs survival**: Ahab's fixation destroys what a sustainable relationship with the ocean could provide
- **Industry and ecology**: the novel documents whaling practices now recognised as catastrophically destructive

## Legacy

The novel was a commercial failure in Melville's lifetime but is now considered one of the greatest American novels. It shaped public perception of whales — particularly [[Sperm Whales]] — and has been cited as an early, unwitting argument for [[Conservation/Threats to Whales|whale conservation]].
